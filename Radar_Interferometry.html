Ground Based Radar Interferometry

in search of short-term glacier velocity variations at Mount Rainier

 

Similar to InSAR (Satellite interferometry), ground based radar interferometry compares two radar images taken at different times from the same place and uses minor shifts in phase to determine offsets between the two time periods with up to mm scale accuracy (unfortunately water vapor and other atmospheric effects can cause some difficulties and reduce sensitivity).


For this project, I worked with David Shean and Steve Malone, both also at UW, as well as Mark Fahnestock at University of Alaska Fairbanks, to use a ground based radar interferometer (GPRI) made by GAMMA (pictured above) to measure glacier velocities at Mount Rainier. This relatively new technology had not been used in this context before.


The main intent of this work was to see if this remote sensing technology could be used to see if there are minor variations in glacier velocity accompanying the appearance of repeating earthquakes that appear when snow falls on the glaciers. This is a major area of research I have been pursuing.


It is not yet clear if this method will be able to resolve such minor velocity variations because we lose time resolution in data processing required to cancel out the strong atmospheric effects present at Mount Rainier. Time will tell, we have collected glacier velocity data from immediately before, during, and immediately after a heavy snowfall (accompanied by repeating earthquakes), but the analysis is still work in progress. But even if it doesn’t help unravel the mystery of repeating earthquakes at Mount Rainier, the results are fascinating for glaciological application. Such high resolution spatial coverage in such an inaccessible and hazardous area is unprecedented.


The map below (mapping credit to David Shean) shows our preliminary results from a pilot study in July 2012. The velocities shown are “line-of-sight” velocities - we can only resolve ground motion directly towards or away from the instrument, but it is a good approximation of overall glacier motion when the ice is moving directly toward the instrument, which is the case for most of the Nisqually and Emmons glaciers. The instrument was located on a road lookout just before Paradise Visitors Center to image the Nisqually and Wilson glaciers (off the bottom of the map) and on a service road just west of Sunrise Visitors Center (off Northeast corner of map) to image the Emmons and Winthrop glaciers. The results are draped over a LiDAR digital elevation model available at the Puget Sound LiDAR Consortium.
